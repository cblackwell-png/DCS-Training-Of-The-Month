Why this training, why now

The context behind putting anti-harassment first in our monthly rotation.

"How we treat each other on the way to the pitch shapes how our poet-athletes see themselves treated on it. A respectful workplace is not a compliance box — it is our mission, lived out among ourselves."

1 in 3

Working women report experiencing workplace sexual harassment — a conservative figure; credible estimates run considerably higher.

3 in 4

Of people who experience harassment at work never report it to a manager, supervisor, or union representative.

Bystanders matter

Bystander intervention is one of the most promising ways to stop harassment — which is why it's built into this month's course.

Sources: U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, Select Task Force on the Study of Harassment in the Workplace (2016), which reports that roughly three in four people who experience harassment never report it, and endorses bystander-intervention training as a prevention tool.

DC-specific context & support

The LinkedIn course covers the shared foundation. These resources add the DC frame — the laws that protect you here, and where to turn for support beyond DC SCORES.

The law

DC Human Rights Act

DC has some of the strongest workplace protections in the country — 21 protected traits, more than any federal law. Understanding what's protected is the first step in recognizing when it's not.

Read the summary at OHR
DC toolkit

OHR Sexual Harassment Prevention Resources

The DC Office of Human Rights maintains a free toolkit with policy templates, training resources, and guidance for employers and employees. A useful DC-specific companion to this month's LinkedIn training.

Open the OHR toolkit
Free legal help

Legal Aid Society of DC

Free legal assistance for DC workers facing workplace discrimination, harassment, or retaliation. Confidential consultations for eligible clients.

Visit Legal Aid DC
DC Bar

DC Bar Pro Bono Center

Attorney referral and pro bono legal services for a range of workplace issues. Useful if Legal Aid income limits don't apply to your situation.

DC Bar Pro Bono Center
Federal option

EEOC Washington Field Office

Federal enforcement for Title VII discrimination and harassment claims. Complaints must be filed within 180 or 300 days depending on circumstances — do not wait.

EEOC DC field office
Crisis support

DC Rape Crisis Center

24/7 confidential support for anyone in the DMV impacted by sexual violence — including workplace incidents. Free, no reporting requirement.

dcrcc.org · 202-333-7273

Retaliation is prohibited. Anyone who reports in good faith — or supports someone who does — is protected under DC SCORES policy and DC law. If you are worried about retaliation, name that worry when you report; we address it directly.

Common questions

Quick answers to the things people ask most. Still stuck? Reach out to People & Culture.

I've completed this training, or one like it, before — do I need to do it again?
Yes. This is the first time we're making it a shared requirement for everyone on staff, so we're all completing it together now — even if you've taken something similar in the past. Going forward it becomes an annual refresh, keeping the whole team on the same, up-to-date footing.
How do I log in to LinkedIn Learning?
Use your dcscores.org account. Click "Start on LinkedIn Learning" above and sign in with your work email — you'll be routed through single sign-on. If you hit a wall, email People & Culture and we'll get you access.
I need an accommodation to complete this training.
We've got you. Whether it's captions, extra time, a different format, or anything else, contact People & Culture and we'll arrange an alternative that works for you — no need to share details you're not comfortable sharing.
What if I can't finish by the deadline?
Let us know before the date if you can. Deadlines keep us all accountable, but life happens — email People & Culture and we'll sort out a plan.
